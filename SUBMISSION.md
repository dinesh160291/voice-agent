## Stage 6: Turn-Taking And Barge-In

Expected evidence (runbook) ŌĆö observed:

- [x] Adaptive calibration reports noise floor and speech threshold in the vad-readout.
- [x] Sustained caller speech cancels playback and records a barge-in:
  `barge_in.candidate` ŌåÆ `barge_in.detected` in the browser event feed, and
  `barge_in.turn_started` server-side on turns 2, 4, 5, 7 of `demo-traces.json`.
- [x] The interrupting turn stays in the same session; the barge-in metric updates once
  (~201 ms detection ŌĆö identical under mock and both live providers, confirming the
  detector is client-side and provider-independent).
- [x] Endpoint slider sweep ŌĆö same sentence with a deliberate mid-sentence pause
  ("I need a room for next weekendŌĆ” actually, make that three nights"), spoken at two
  endpoint settings, then restored to 650 ms:

  **At 350 ms ŌĆö the pause ends the turn, and the damage compounds:**

  ```text
  caller: I need a room for next weekend.          <- cut at the pause
  agent:  Could you please provide me with your check-in and check-out dates ...
  Caller interrupted agent playback                 <- my own continuation became a barge-in
  caller: Can you make that three nights?           <- fragment without context
  agent:  I still need your check-in and check-out dates ...   <- conversation went backwards
  ```

  Two turns (EOT-to-audio 2,847 ms and 3,505 ms, the second flagged
  `barge_in.turn_started`), ~6.4 s combined dead air, zero information successfully
  communicated. An over-eager endpoint doesn't just truncate audio ŌĆö it fragments
  *meaning*, and each fragment costs a full round-trip of the whole cascade.

  **At 900 ms ŌĆö the pause survives:** one uninterrupted turn, transcript
  "I need a room for the next weekend. Actually, make it for three nights.", no barge-in
  event, EOT-to-first-audio 5,057 ms (STT 1,166 + LLM 1,633 + TTS 1,925 + transport) ŌĆö
  the trade is the longer wait after truly finishing.

  **Field note from repeated runs:** even at longer endpoint settings, a natural
  mid-sentence pause frequently loses the race ŌĆö the agent starts answering and I have to
  barge in to finish the request. Endpointing by silence alone cannot tell "thinking pause"
  from "done talking"; the barge-in path is what makes the mistake recoverable. Short
  endpoint = fast but cuts callers off; long endpoint = patient but adds dead air; 650 ms
  is the compromise default, and production systems add semantic end-of-turn detection on
  top (deck: the four-state turn model) precisely because this race is unwinnable on
  silence alone.

## Beyond the runbook 1: Latency tuning study

Same agent, different `.env` lines only:

| Cascade configuration | STT | LLM | TTS | EOT-to-first-audio |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Mock (plumbing floor) | 0 | 0 | 0 (browser) | **28 ms** |
| OpenAI defaults (`whisper-1`, `tts-1`) | 3,819 | ~1,257 | ~1,100-7,229* | 6,489-9,499 |
| OpenAI tuned (`gpt-4o-mini-transcribe`, `gpt-4o-mini-tts`) | 710-1,102 | ~1,200-1,800 | ~2,830* | ~6,078 |

\* TTS synthesis scales with reply length (the 7,229 ms sample was a ~65-word room catalog).
In a non-streaming bridge the entire reply is synthesized before the first audio byte ships,
so verbose replies convert directly into caller dead air ŌĆö observable in the demo as my own
barge-ins on long answers.

Findings:

1. Latency is model/serving time, not plumbing (28 ms floor with AI mocked out).
2. Tuning a sequential cascade is whack-a-mole: fixing STT (3,819 ŌåÆ 710 ms, one env line)
   promoted TTS to the new bottleneck; the total is always the sum of stages.
3. The sum has a ~3-4 s floor in this request/response architecture. Getting under it
   requires overlap ŌĆö stream LLM tokens into sentence-chunked TTS and push audio chunks
   while generation continues, with barge-in cancelling all in-flight streams. That is the
   room-native streaming architecture (LiveKit Agents / OpenAI Realtime API); identified as
   the path to production rather than built here.
4. Candidate next optimization, gated behind evals per the runbook's rule: a response-brevity
   budget in the system prompt ŌĆö reply length is now the dominant controllable cost.
